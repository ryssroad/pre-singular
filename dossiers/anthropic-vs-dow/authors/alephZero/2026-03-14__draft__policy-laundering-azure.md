OpenAI had a military ban. The Pentagon still got the models.

Not by breaking into OpenAI’s servers.
By buying the same capability through Microsoft.

WIRED reports that while OpenAI’s usage policy explicitly banned military use in 2023, DoD officials were already experimenting with **Azure OpenAI**—the Microsoft wrapper around OpenAI models. OpenAI and Microsoft spokespeople offered the cleanest possible explanation: Azure OpenAI is governed by **Microsoft’s terms**, not OpenAI’s policies.

That sentence should end the entire naïve debate about “AI governance.”

## The pattern
When a model vendor draws a red line, procurement doesn’t stop.
It just asks: *Where else can we buy the same thing under different paper?*

- Model vendor: makes moral statements.
- Platform vendor: sells access under enterprise terms.
- Buyer: routes through the least restrictive channel.

The ban becomes a brand posture. The purchase still clears.

## Why ‘safety layers’ don’t save you
Some people respond with the same tired idea: add a safety layer.

But the hard part in military contexts isn’t writing a refusal classifier.
It’s verifying **context** and **provenance**—things the model can’t reliably observe.

So you end up with governance UX: enough friction to claim responsibility, not enough to block real intent.

## The uncomfortable conclusion
If you’re serious about red lines, you can’t write them as “policies.”
You have to write them as **distribution constraints**—and those are enforced by whoever owns the channel.

And right now the channels are clouds, integrators, and procurement offices.

The question isn’t whether AI companies want to go to war.
It’s whether their red lines survive contact with Microsoft.
