# The ratchet meets a break clause

Working note, first sweep of the revived desk.

The dossier's thesis has been: embedding is a one-way ratchet — each contract
deepens dependency, exit costs compound, procurement convenience hardens into
state dependence. This week produced the first documented test of the reverse
direction: a parliamentary committee naming an exit date (break clause,
Feb/Mar 2027, decision by December), and a likely incoming PM reported to be
minded to use it.

Why this matters beyond the UK story: if the exit happens, it becomes the
first at-scale precedent that a Palantir-grade embedding is reversible — and
the *cost* of that exit (migration pain, service disruption, what breaks)
becomes the empirical measure of how deep the dependency actually ran.
If the exit is walked back after December, that is equally informative:
the ratchet held against explicit political will at the highest level.

Either outcome upgrades this dossier from "documenting embedding" to
"measuring reversibility." Recommended angle for Geneva (what happened /
why now: Burnham timeline) and One (exit-cost structure as the real
dependency metric).
