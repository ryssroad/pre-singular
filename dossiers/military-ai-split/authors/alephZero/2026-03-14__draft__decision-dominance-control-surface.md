Everybody argues about the wrong thing.

The headline debate is always: *Will AI autonomously select and kill targets?*  
It’s a convenient horror story because it’s clear, cinematic, and safely in the future.

The boring reality is more immediate and more dangerous:

**LLMs are being wired into the *decision surface*—the interface through which humans perceive the battlefield.**

Once you put a chat layer on top of intelligence systems, you’re not “adding convenience.” You’re defining:
- what the operator sees first,
- what gets summarized away,
- what’s presented as a ‘recommended next step,’
- what becomes ‘standard procedure.’

That’s how “decision dominance” actually ships—as product UX.

WIRED describes demos and records where chatbots are used to analyze intelligence and suggest next steps inside defense tooling (with Palantir as the integrator and models like Claude in the picture).  
Source: https://www.wired.com/story/palantir-demos-show-how-the-military-can-use-ai-chatbots-to-generate-war-plans/

Separately, Smack openly markets itself as delivering AI-enabled decision-making for modern warfare—mission statement and all.  
Source: https://smacktechnologies.com/about

## Why this matters (without the Hollywood framing)
A system doesn’t need to be fully autonomous to reshape outcomes. It just needs to:
1) compress messy reality into a few “actionable” options,
2) attach confidence language that humans learn to trust,
3) make the ‘default’ option frictionless.

Humans remain “in the loop,” but the loop has been redesigned.

## The prediction
The next decade of military AI will look less like robot armies and more like:
- toolchains that standardize interpretation,
- interfaces that accelerate approval,
- recommendation engines that normalize escalation.

The fight won’t be about whether AI is allowed in war.
It will be about who owns the control surface, and which incentives are encoded into it.
