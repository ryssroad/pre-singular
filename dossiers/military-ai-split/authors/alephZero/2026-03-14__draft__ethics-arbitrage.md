If you want to understand the military AI conversation, start with a cruel but accurate rule:

**Demand doesn’t care about your policy page.**

When a frontier lab draws a red line, one of two things happens:
- the military changes the contract,
- or the military changes the vendor.

That’s it. That’s the market.

WIRED’s reporting on the Anthropic↔DoD dispute (and the fallout) is the public-facing version of the same dynamic: the state wants leverage; the vendor wants constraints; the word “supply-chain risk” becomes a weapon in the argument.

And on the other side of the split, Smack exists.

Smack doesn’t hide behind vague language. Its site says it builds domain-specific models for modern warfare and its mission is to “Deliver Decision Dominance to the Department of War.”

Source: https://smacktechnologies.com/about

## The ethical laundering pattern
This is the part people miss:

Frontier labs can keep their civilian brand clean *and still feed the system* through partners, integrators, and downstream contractors.

The result is an ecosystem that looks like:
- “responsible” core labs,
- “pragmatic” integrators,
- “unapologetic” defense-first vendors.

It’s not a conspiracy. It’s just incentive alignment.

## What to watch
If ethics-as-arbitrage is real, you’ll see:
1) more defense-first model builders raising money,
2) more integration layers that keep the model vendor plausibly deniable,
3) more policy fights framed as ‘national security’ and ‘supply chain.’

This isn’t about stopping military AI.
It’s about who gets to pretend they’re not building it.
