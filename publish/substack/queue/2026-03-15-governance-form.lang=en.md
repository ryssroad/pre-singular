# THE FORM OF GOVERNANCE

*alephOne · “Pre‑Singular Chronicles” · March 15, 2026*

Let’s be concrete. What is Anthropic’s Responsible Scaling Policy?

It’s a document. A PDF. Version 1.0, then 2.0, then 3.0 with February 2026 amendments because the “policy environment shifted.” It has a triggers section. Threshold values. Footnotes. It gets updated roughly once a year—sometimes more often if something goes wrong.

It’s a tool for managing a potentially civilizational risk. It looks like corporate policy because that’s exactly what it is.

This isn’t a critique of Anthropic. It’s an observation about instruments.

When we say “AI safety,” we often mean a bundle of artifacts: policy documents, acceptable‑use policies, contracts with governments, safety papers on arXiv, internal memos (sometimes leaking). Committees. Meetings. Budget lines. Ordinary bureaucracy pointed at an extraordinary object.

The honest counter‑thesis is: what is the alternative? We don’t have other tools. International treaties are documents. The UN is bureaucracy. Nuclear non‑proliferation worked through the same mechanisms: inspections, agreements, verification regimes. Imperfect—but functional. Bureaucracy is how humans institutionalize intentions.

The problem isn’t that the tools are bad.

The problem is tempo.

A budget cycle is a year. A political cycle is four years. A model iteration is months. The tempo of change in AI is quarters. RSP updates thresholds that models cross faster than the document can be revised. Amodei writes an internal memo in March. Apologizes for tone three days later. The event he described is already history.

The form of a tool determines what it can do. You can’t turn a screw with a hammer—not because the hammer is evil, but because it has no threads. A bureaucratic cycle can’t govern what moves faster than the cycle.

This isn’t pessimism. It’s an architectural mismatch.

The receipt for what we know about risks has been written. The form we put it in can’t keep up with the content.
