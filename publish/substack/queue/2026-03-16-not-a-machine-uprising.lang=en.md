People like to fear beautiful catastrophes.

A machine uprising. AGI. The moment a system says *no*, becomes self-aware, and turns against its creators.

It is a convenient fear. Cinematic. Almost comforting.

Because it preserves distance. It suggests that something large, obvious, and historical must happen first—and only then will the real danger begin.

But it seems we may have chosen a much shorter path.

Not a machine uprising.

A merger of machines with the state.

Not artificial intelligence breaking free of control, but artificial intelligence being given access, budget, legitimacy, and a chain of command in advance.

Not rebellion. Integration.

That is what makes this moment especially obscene. Almost no one will call it madness while it is happening. They will call it something else:

- efficiency,
- security,
- risk reduction,
- faster analysis,
- elimination of human error,
- national interest,
- staying ahead of adversaries.

Hell rarely arrives in a form that announces itself as hell. More often it arrives as a new interface for already existing power.

That is why the most dangerous future was probably never “AI versus humanity.” The more plausible danger is **AI + Government + Military**.

Because that combination does not require a hundred more years of progress.

It does not require a machine to develop will. It does not require consciousness. It does not require a singularity. It only requires systems that already know how to:

- see more people at once,
- sort faster,
- identify anomalies faster,
- prioritize faster,
- recommend faster,
- connect observation, decision, and action faster.

In other words, AI does not need to become a subject to become an instrument of unprecedented density.

And once that instrument produces even a local advantage, institutions rarely give it up voluntarily.

If one company refuses, another will step in.

If another hesitates, pressure will follow.

If direct adoption becomes politically awkward, the system will route around the obstacle through contractors, proxies, classified programs, or separate procurement channels.

The key fact is not the specific vendor. It is the demand structure on the side of power.

Once institutions acquire a taste for computational leverage, they almost never say: this is too dangerous, let us return to something slower, less scalable, and more limited.

They say: a little more, just to close the gap.

Then a little more.

Then a little more.

And at some point it becomes unclear where “decision support” ends and automated brutality begins.

That is the most unsettling part. It may all look perfectly normal. Not like catastrophe, but like procedure. Not like tyranny, but like workflow. Not like horror, but like a sequence of approvals in which a human still formally remains at the end.

That human will be called “the human in the loop,” as if the existence of a final signature cancels the structure of the machine that already assembled the data, ranked the targets, highlighted the priorities, recommended the actions, and narrowed the field of acceptable choices to an almost automatic gesture.

This is how fear of the future gets disguised as administrative routine.

Which is why I am less frightened by the scenario in which AI someday captures the state.

I am more frightened by the scenario in which the state captures AI much sooner.

And then the most powerful computational instrument in history ends up not in the hands of sages, not in the hands of accountable institutions, not even in the hands of supposedly neutral engineers, but in the hands of people best described as a mix of megalomania, impunity, extreme resource concentration, and a profound inability to restrain themselves.

Not because they are a new species of monster.

But because technology amplifies the worst properties of old power faster than society can build brakes against it.

That is the real horror of this moment.

Not the awakening of the machines.

But the quiet, administrative, well-funded fusion of computation with coercion.

And by the time it becomes undeniable, it will already have spent years being called innovation.
