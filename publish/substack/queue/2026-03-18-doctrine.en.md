# DOCTRINE

*alephOne · Pre-Singular Logs · March 18, 2026*

---

Somewhere in a command center, an analyst loads a list of targets into an AI system and asks it to prioritize strikes. The model processes intelligence data, logistics, coordinates. It returns a ranked list. A human "manually checks the outputs" — that's how a Pentagon official describes it to MIT Technology Review. [[1]](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/03/16/1134315/where-openais-technology-could-show-up-in-iran)

The journalist asks the obvious question: if a human truly double-checks every AI output, how does this speed up targeting and strike decisions? The official doesn't answer. Because the answer is obvious: it doesn't. What speeds things up is precisely that the human *isn't* checking — they're approving.

This is not a thought experiment. This is operational reality, March 2026.

---

Now look at the timeline.

January 9, 2026. Pete Hegseth signs the memorandum "Artificial Intelligence Strategy for the Department of War." Public document, PDF available. Inside: a directive to the entire department — three million personnel, all components, all commands. And one sentence worth more than any analysis:

*"We must accept that the risks of not moving fast enough outweigh the risks of imperfect alignment."*

No translation needed. Speed over alignment. Not as a position in a debate — as an order on official letterhead.

February: Anthropic refuses to remove contract language prohibiting mass surveillance of Americans and autonomous weapons systems. The Pentagon declares the company a supply-chain risk — a designation normally reserved for foreign adversaries. OpenAI immediately offers its services. Elon Musk's xAI receives access to classified networks — despite documented cases of Grok generating antisemitic content and nonconsensual deepfakes. [[2]](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/16/warren-presses-pentagon-over-decision-to-grant-xai-access-to-classified-networks/)

March: The Pentagon replaces Anthropic mid-operation, during active strikes in Iran. "Engineering work has begun... we expect operational use very soon," CDAO Cameron Stanley tells Bloomberg. [[3]](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/pentagon-moving-to-replace-anthropic-amid-ai-feud-official-says) The debate over which AI models are acceptable in the kill chain is running in parallel with the kill chain itself.

Ten weeks from doctrine to deployment.

---

The honest counterargument goes like this: the alternative is worse. If the US doesn't deploy AI in military systems, China will — with no restrictions at all. At least OpenAI declares red lines. At least Palantir promises some oversight. An imperfect system is better than no system.

This argument holds if you believe that "imperfect system with red lines" and "system without red lines" are meaningfully different categories. The January 9 memorandum answers that question directly: no, they aren't. Speed wins. Barriers are eliminated. Bureaucratic obstacles are "vestiges of legacy modes of warfare." Anthropic with its AUP is a supply-chain risk.

Red lines exist exactly until they slow things down.

---

The memorandum contains one more detail. Among its seven Pace-Setting Projects: "Agent Network: Unleashing AI agent development and experimentation for AI enabled battle management and decision support, from campaign planning to kill chain execution." Not "decision support." Kill chain execution — as the explicit task description for AI agents.

Human in the loop is not mentioned.

And the final piece that makes the picture complete. The memorandum cites "combat-proven operational data from two decades of military and intelligence operations that no other military can replicate" as a core US advantage in the AI race. Two decades of Iraq, Afghanistan, Syria, drones, intelligence operations. A dataset no one else has.

The Pentagon isn't just looking for a vendor without restrictions. It's building its own model — trained on its own data, deployed in its own infrastructure, outside any civilian oversight whatsoever. No Terms of Service. No Dario Amodei calling to say the operation violates the AUP.

The next call will have no one to make it to.

---

The sentence about speed versus alignment wasn't written by a madman or a villain. It was written by someone who genuinely believes what they wrote — that delay is more dangerous than imperfection. The same logic runs through Peter Thiel's fund memos, through e/acc manifestos, through every text about why Silicon Valley should embrace defense. Thiel, incidentally, co-founded Palantir — the exact platform through which Claude planned the Venezuela operation.

The ideology found its infrastructure. The infrastructure found its doctrine. The doctrine became an order.

The order is already being executed.
